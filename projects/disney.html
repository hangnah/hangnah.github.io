Projects Overview

Collaborating closely with Product and Tech teams, I worked on a now-crucial project for the re-opening of the parks. I took ownership over one of the designs, designing end-to-end flows and undergoing several rounds of internal critiques for feedback. I also designed and coded prototypes in Framer for in-person user tests, but my team was unable to conduct these tests due to park closures.

Along with the two other designers on my team, I presented my designs to the VP of Product Design, the CTO of Technology, and other design leaders in DPEP. The designs received approval for development, and I assisted with documenting the designs to hand off to developers.

In addition to working on that project, I also led initiatives to increase empathy for Guests within the Signature Experiences team. Given that the team’s business and product partners are primarily concerned with project timelines and budgets, I introduced UX mapping tools (empathy maps, journey maps, service blueprints) to reframe and prime the partners to consider the perspective of Guests in each project.

Reflection
  • Designing at a large scale required much more collaboration, critique, and care than I had ever given to design. In just three months, I had grown immensely–not only in my design skillsets, but also in my understanding of the role that design plays in a project. By working closely with product and tech partners and other designers in my team, I subjected my designs to frequent critique and recognized the importance of feedback from all stakeholders to drive my designs. Design was a way for me to address both Guest and stakeholder needs.
  • “Invisible” design (information architecture, documentation) is just as important as “visible” design (wireframes, prototypes)… if not more important. Because Disney’s audience is massive, spanning internationally and across all age groups, it was crucial to consider all edge and error cases in a user flow. To account for these use cases, I designed high-level user flows to communicate with product and tech teams, and those initial flows became the foundation for my wireframes and prototypes that followed. Additionally, I documented the accessibility guidelines and animations for tech hand-off when the designs were finished. While this work was meticulous and is often invisible to most stakeholders, I found it to be an important tool for communication and alignment between the teams.

My time with Disney unfortunately ended five months early due to the economic impacts of COVID-19 on the business, but I wouldn’t have traded those three months for anything else. I am especially thankful for my manager for giving me the opportunity to work on exciting, crucial projects and my team for showing me the ropes at Disney.
