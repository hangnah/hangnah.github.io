As an analysis of ethical surveillance in my human-computer interaction class, my team of three other UCLA graduate students researched the ethics of data collection and design values of ridesharing applications, specifically in Uber. We applied concepts of personas, infrastructural inversion, and values in design to complete our analysis, and proposed three design solutions that reflected our findings.

This project was a part of a graduate-level human-computer interaction class in the Information Studies department (INF STD 272) at UCLA. Our class was taught by Professor Leah Leivrouw.

Research & Persona

In many major cities, ridesharing applications bring much convenience and affordability that other transportation services cannot offer. However, in order to do so, these ridesharing companies must have a record of user location data and traffic patterns to streamline their services – heavy location surveillance that some individuals may not be aware of. With this issue of ethical surveillance in ridesharing applications in mind, my team used personas, situations, and infrastructural inversion* to uncover our design values that would help us analyze Uber’s design decisions and come to our own design decisions.

*Infrastructural inversion is a phrase coined by Star and Bowker in their book Sorting Things Out. The process of infrastructural inversion involves diving into the infrastructure (or classification) and analyzing the details of the product at hand.

Infrastructural Inversion
FAMILIARIZATION

My team familiarized ourselves with the interface of the Uber application and found it to be seamless, intuitive, and legible, leading us to believe that Uber values the usability, simplicity, and convenience of its application by hiding the inner-workings of the service. Uber does allow users to opt-in or -out of their data collection services and links to their full privacy policy online, but this affordance doesn’t allow transparency with how Uber uses the data collected from its users (for example, Uber selling data to generate revenue with partners).

DEFAMILIARIZATION

How does Uber get away with collecting tons of data without worrying its users? In order to answer this, my team used defamiliarization to examine the navigational cues that lead Uber’s users throughout the application. With the primary purpose of Uber being to request a ride, this one-track mindset is evident via the design of the landing screen, not allowing its users to strongly consider the data collection settings or their location privacies. The simplicity and intuitive nature of Uber’s design enables its users to forgo the constant location surveillance and data collection happening under the hood of the application.

Persona

We developed a persona to reflect an individual who is cognizant of Uber’s location tracking services but unaware of how Uber uses this data, making her uneasy with using Uber as a ride service.

Design Features

Design Values

Drawing from our infrastructural inversion and an article on surveillance capitalism by Zuboff (2015), we defined five of the following design values needed for ethical surveillance. These values respond to Uber’s concern with efficiency and usability, and also align with user values in data collection and location surveillance. We used these values to drive our design decisions.

Each team member was responsible for one design feature. I was responsible for the Route Selection feature.

  • Route Selection
  • Uber Pool Express Selection and Rating
  • Video Privacy

Uber drivers have the option of using different navigation applications (Uber Nav, Waze, Google Maps). Each application has its own algorithm for calculating the fastest route for travel, which can lead drivers to take their riders through more obscure neighborhoods to get to their destinations. The proposed redesign would allow riders to preview all the possible routes and select one before submitting a request for a ride.

VALUES: TRANSPARENCY, SAFETY, AGENCY

The Uber Pool Express option determines a “smart spot” for riders to be picked up and dropped off, but ignores other factors like the safety and comfortability of riders. This proposed design would allow riders to rate their drop-off and pick-up locations after the ride, improving Uber’s algorithm and giving agency to future riders in choosing a ride with a particular pick-up or drop-off location.

VALUES: TRANSPARENCY, ACCOUNTABILITY, SAFETY, AGENCY

Uber drivers have a right to record their rides using dashboard cameras both in and outside of their vehicles, and more drivers have been installing camera devices to protect themselves from false claims. Riders are often unaware of this, so in order to inform riders of the possibility of being recorded during a particular ride, we proposed having a opt-in or -out dialog in the rider’s application before each ride with a camera device. This would also require drivers to register their devices with the Uber application.

VALUES: TRANSPARENCY, PRIVACY, AGENCY

Scenarios

Given our constraints for this project, we were unable to complete user testing for our design proposals. Instead, we used our persona and created scenarios for her in order to apply our designs.

  • Scenario 01: Uber Pool
  • Scenario 02: Data Privacy

Stephanie just completed a work trip to Chicago. She is at Reagan National airport and it is after midnight. Stephanie needs to get home quickly to her apartment in downtown Washington, DC. The bus and metro have closed for the night, and the line for taxis seems to be taking at least 20 minutes. The fastest and cheapest option would be to call an Uber carpool to get home. Stephanie hesitates to call an UberPool. The last time she took an UberPool, she was dropped off in an unsafe part of her neighborhood. Stephanie does not want this to happen again, especially because it is so late at night.

Solution: Stephanie can use the UberPool rating system to select her desired drop off spot, or use the route selection tool to choose a familiar route home.

Stephanie reads an article online about Uber riders who have installed cameras in their cars in order to film passengers. This concerns Stephanie—who has video footage of her? How many drivers are conducting video surveillance on their riders?

Solution: Stephanie can use the video privacy settings in her Uber application to prescreen which drivers will have cameras installed in their cars. Additionally, she can quickly review her video privacy settings through the application’s home page.

Reflection

In all the design proposals my team came up with, the common value we upheld was agency – being able to control what types of data that is collected and knowing how the data is being used. Regarding ethical surveillance, I believe that having agency over how our personal data is collected leads us to feel more empowered by corporations that leverage big data without our active knowledge. Also, given our short timeline for the project, we didn’t fully flesh out the tradeoffs for the design propositions; after presenting our project to our class, my team and I recognized the biases that riders could potentially contribute to through the proposed rating system or route selection designs. Digging further into these concepts would help us combat any forms of bias that we may be contributing to in our design. Overall, this practice of critically thinking about existing pervasive technologies and the surveillance that accompanies them opened my eyes to examine other mindless interactions I have with technologies, growing my interest in research and analysis as a designer.
