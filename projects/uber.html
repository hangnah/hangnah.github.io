As an analysis of ethical surveillance in my human-computer interaction class, my team of three other UCLA graduate students researched the ethics of data collection and design values of ridesharing applications, specifically in Uber. We applied concepts of personas, infrastructural inversion, and values in design to complete our analysis, and proposed three design solutions that reflected our findings.

This project was a part of a graduate-level human-computer interaction class in the Information Studies department (INF STD 272) at UCLA. Our class was taught by Professor Leah Leivrouw.

Problem & Research

How might we make location surveillance more ethical?

In many major cities, ridesharing applications are convenient and offer affordances that other transportation services cannot offer. In order to do so, however, these ridesharing applications keep records of user location data and traffic patterns to streamline their services – performing heavy location surveillance that individuals may not be aware of. With this issue of ethical surveillance in ridesharing applications in mind, my team used personas, crafted situations, and conducted infrastructural inversion* to uncover our design values that would help us analyze Uber’s design decisions and come to our own design decisions to address the issue of ethical surveillance.

* Infrastructural inversion is a phrase coined by Star and Bowker in their book Sorting Things Out. The process of infrastructural inversion involves diving into the infrastructure and analyzing the details of the product at hand.

Infrastructural Inversion

Analyzing Uber’s interface revealed several design values that may have driven their decisions to hide their heavy location surveillance.

Familiarization

My team familiarized ourselves with Uber’s mobile application interface and found it to be seamless, intuitive, and legible, leading us to believe that Uber champions the usability, simplicity, and convenience of its application to hide their location surveillance. Although Uber does allow users to opt-in or -out of their data collection services and provides links to their full privacy policies online, this affordance doesn’t provide transparency with how Uber uses the data collected from its users (for example, Uber selling data to generate revenue with partners).

Defamiliarization

How does Uber get away with collecting tons of data without worrying its users? In order to answer this, my team used defamiliarization to examine the navigational cues that lead Uber’s users throughout the application. Given Uber’s primary goal of requesting a ride, the design of the application seamlessly leads users through the ride-requesting process from the application’s first launch to the final push notification from a driver. This flow, however, discourages users from strongly considering their data collection settings, subsequently contributing to their indifference of their location privacies. The simplicity of Uber’s design enables its users to forgo the constant location surveillance and data collection happening under the hood of the application.

Persona

In order to explore this issue further, my team developed a persona to reflect an individual who is cognizant of Uber’s location tracking services but unaware of how Uber uses this data, making her uneasy with using Uber as a ride service.

Design

Using our proposed design values for ethical surveillance, each team member devised a solution to address ethical surveillance in Uber’s mobile application.

Drawing from our infrastructural inversion and an article on surveillance capitalism by Zuboff (2015), we defined five design values that contribute to ethical location surveillance. These values align with Uber’s values of efficiency and usability, and also respond to user concerns with data collection and location surveillance. These values were used to drive our design decisions.

Each team member was responsible for one design feature. I was responsible for the Route Selection feature.

  • Route Selection
  • Express Pool Selection and Rating
  • Video Privacy

Uber drivers have the option of using different navigation applications when completing a ride (Uber Nav, Waze, Google Maps). Each application has its own algorithm for calculating the fastest route for travel, which can guide drivers through obscure neighborhoods or unsafe road conditions to get to their destinations. The proposed redesign would allow riders to preview all the possible routes and select one before submitting a request for a ride.

Values: Transparency, Safety, Agency

The current Express Pool option determines a “smart spot” for riders to be picked up and dropped off to optimize the driver’s route, but ignores other factors including the safety of riders. This proposed design would allow riders to rate their drop-off and pick-up locations after the ride, improving Uber’s algorithm and giving agency to future riders for choosing rides with higher-rated pick-up or drop-off locations.

Values: Safety, Agency, Transparency, Accountability

Uber drivers have a right to record their rides using dashboard cameras both in and outside of their vehicles, and more drivers have been installing camera devices to protect themselves from false claims. Riders are often unaware of this, so in order to inform riders of the possibility of being recorded during their rides, we proposed having a opt-in or -out dialog in the rider’s application before each ride that has a camera device. A visible button toggle on the landing page would also remind riders of this privacy option. This would require drivers to register their camera devices with the Uber application.

Values: Transparency, Privacy, Agency
Storyboarding

With our persona as the primary user, I designed storyboards to validate and test our design solutions.

Given our constraints and timeline for this project, we were unable to complete user testing for our design proposals. Instead, we used our persona and created scenarios for her in order to apply our designs.

My team fabricated these scenarios together. After the project was completed, I revised the scenarios and drew the following storyboards to illustrate the scenarios.

  • Scenario 1: Uber Pool
  • Scenario 2: Data Privacy and Rating

Stephanie just completed a work trip to Chicago. She is at Reagan National airport and it is after midnight. Stephanie needs to get home quickly to her apartment in downtown Washington, DC. The bus and metro have closed for the night, and the line for taxis seems to be taking at least 20 minutes. The fastest and cheapest option would be to call an Uber carpool to get home. Stephanie hesitates to call an UberPool. The last time she took an UberPool, she was dropped off in an unsafe part of her neighborhood. Stephanie does not want this to happen again, especially because it is so late at night.

Solution: Stephanie can use the UberPool rating system to select her desired drop off spot, or use the route selection tool to choose a familiar route home.

Stephanie reads an article online about Uber riders who have installed cameras in their cars in order to film passengers. This concerns Stephanie—who has video footage of her? How many drivers are conducting video surveillance on their riders?

Solution: Stephanie can use the video privacy settings in her Uber application to pre-screen which drivers will have cameras installed in their cars. Additionally, she can quickly review her video privacy settings through the application’s home page.

Reflection

In all the design proposals my team came up with, the common value we upheld was agency – being able to control what types of data that is collected and knowing how the data is being used. Regarding ethical surveillance, I believe that having agency over how our personal data is collected leads us to feel more empowered by corporations that leverage big data without our active knowledge.

After a personal post-mortem reflection, I recognized flaws in our design solutions. Given the short timeline that my team had for this project, we were unable to fully flesh out the tradeoffs for our design propositions. One particular issue that should be addressed is the negative bias that riders could potentially contribute to in certain neighborhoods by rating the safety of routes or pick-up and drop-off locations. Spending more time digging into those proposed solutions would help us uncover ways to combat potential negative biases.

Overall, I enjoyed this practice of critically examining pervasive technologies. Surveillance of all forms is constant, and while it may provide convenience and contribute to better user experiences, it is worth taking a step back to analyze the ethics of these technologies. This project opened my eyes to consider other mindless interactions I have with technologies, growing my interest in research and analysis as a designer.
