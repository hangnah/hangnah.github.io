Research & Ideation

How can online learning be more interactive and engaging?

Fulcrum Labs is revolutionizing the way learners achieve mastery in various industries with its machine learning platform. Using data-driven analytics, the platform presents learners with material based on their performance throughout their courses. Learners are able to learn at their own pace and are presented with either text or video modules, and complete assessments at the end of each course section.

Part of my research for this project stemmed from a psychology course on perception and learning that I took at UCLA. Perceptual learning is fostered through repeated exposure and experience with the presented stimuli, and these skills help form the foundation for other complex cognitive processes such as language processing and decision making. For example, perceptual learning can explain why an individual’s physical learning environment affects his or her learning, as he or she will be better equipped to respond in that particular environment more than other environments. Additionally, note-taking contributes to better information processing, as it allows individuals to visually organize materials and reduces cognitive load during learning.

The existing interface for Fulcrum Lab’s learning product was simple and conducive to learning, but I wanted to explore the addition of interactive elements to increase learner engagement with the platform and retention of material. Therefore, my objective was to introduce a note-taking system and a dashboard customization feature to make learning more engaging for the user.

Design I

I designed a native note-taking feature and created templates for learners to customize their dashboards.

The design proposals needed to be native in the current platform, so I introduced in-app changes to the dashboard interface. I decided to design the note-taking feature directly to the right of the current module for learners to easily take notes as they proceed through the course. I tucked the dashboard customization feature to the bottom of the dashboard, as this feature was cosmetic and I assumed would not contribute to learner engagement and retention as strongly as note-taking would.

  • Note-Taking
  • Dashboard Customization

I designed a native note-taking that would allow learners to directly interact with the text module. Learners would be able to interact with the text in two ways: highlighting a section of text or adding a note to a section of text. In order to access these options, learners would have to highlight a section of text with their cursors, in which the “Highlight” and “Add Note” icons would toggle from disabled to enabled. After the learners highlight or add a note, an indicator would appear to the right of the text as a reference point to their notes. Additionally, I designed a separate “Notes” page for learners to access all of their notes in the course.

I designed several themes for the learner dashboard to give learners the option to customize their online learning environment, which aimed to engage learners with the course content. The themes were accessible via a link to a pop-up, as I did not want to direct learners away from the course content.

User Tests & Design II

The note-taking feature wasn’t as intuitive as I had thought, and users had difficulty finding where to customize their themes.

I conducted two in-person user tests with people who had no previous knowledge of Fulcrum Labs. I prompted the users with tasks to gauge the usability of my designs and screen-recorded their interactions with the prototype as I took notes of my observations.

  • Note-Taking
  • Dashboard Customization

Both users were confused with how to take notes. Although both the “Highlight” and “Add Note” icons toggled from disabled to enabled after the users highlighted the text with their cursors, both users navigated only to the highlight button.

Considering this interaction, I decided to make the “Add Notes” button larger and present it on the right side of the screen at all times. Instead of assuming that learners would use their cursors to highlight the text in order to make a note, I labeled the note-taking icon to make this feature more apparent for learners.

When I tasked the two users with customizing their dashboard theme, both of them looked through their account settings before finding the pop-up link at the bottom of the page. Additionally, after the users had found the theme customization pop-up, they chose one of the first few themes without scrolling through the second page of themes.

In order to “onboard” learners on how to customize their dashboards, I decided to gamify the dashboard customization feature by unlocking this feature for learners as they progress through the course. I also reduced the number of available themes, as learners did not seem to prioritize this new feature in their learning experience.

Reflection

Communicate well. Test early and often.

Throughout the design process, I recognized the importance of user testing to either support or undermine my design decisions. In the research and discussion phase of these two ideas, I was relying on assumptions and feedback from the rest of the product team to drive my design decisions. With user testing, I was able to test these assumptions and the usability of my designs. Ultimately, after meeting with the product design team and discussing the viability of my design propositions, we decided to scrap the idea of customization, as it didn’t seem to add as much value to the learning platform as the note-taking feature.

Being able to take ownership of this project from research to testing was exciting, and I am grateful to the product team at Fulcrum Labs for giving me the opportunity to do so. While I learned a great deal about collaborating between the design team and receiving feedback on designs, one thing I would have done differently is communicate my ideas earlier and more often throughout the project. That would have helped me to develop one product feature more thoroughly rather than splitting my focus between two features.

At the end of my internship, I presented and delivered my research, wireframes, and prototypes to the team.
