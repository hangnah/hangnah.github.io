Valour SF

Encouraging San Francisco residents to volunteer at local homeless organizations.

Role

UX Designer

Timeline

24 Hours / Nov 2019

Methods

Personas, Wireframing, Information Architecture, Prototyping

Context

Hackathon, Mobile App

This project was completed at the Girls in SF’s Hacking for Humanity hackathon in November 2019 and received an honorable mention from the judges. I worked in a team with four other people as the only designer.

What does it mean to “Hack for Humanity”?

The goal of the hackathon was to address a social or civic issue in San Francisco. A couple of representatives from different community organizations were invited to speak about their organizations’ respective missions.

One organization stood out to my team – the St. Anthony Foundation, an organization that provides free services to the homeless community in San Francisco.

Defining our users

My team wanted to explore homelessness even further. With over 8,000 homeless individuals and 800,000 residents in San Francisco, we needed to identify the groups involved in this space to narrow our project scope:

Homeless Individuals

Non-Profit Organizations

Volunteers

Initially focusing on homeless individuals as our primary users, we preliminarily discussed a few project ideas, including a service to temporarily house the homeless in open houses. While these ideas were worth exploring, we were concerned about the barrier of technology access among homeless individuals.

Because of this, we shifted our users from homeless individuals to people who have the resources to contribute to the homeless community.

Ideating to increase compassion

We considered the idea of bridging relationships between San Francisco residents and the homeless community (similar to a sponsorship program), but recognized the potential hesitation that might be felt by residents from participating in an independent program.

Pictured: Initial whiteboarding session

We decided to leverage organizations like St. Anthony’s to establish the foundations of these relationships. My team began considering ways to encourage people to volunteer at homeless organizations in order to ultimately foster relationships with homeless individuals.

Bouncing around ideas for this was difficult, as my team members and I had differing solutions. I suggested coming up with user personas to narrow down the scope and help direct our decisions for the project.

Developing user personas

My team was not familiar with human-centered design principles, so I led my team to create profiles for three individuals living in San Francisco. We defined general perspectives on volunteering and considered users who were not previously involved with volunteer programs. (The actual personas were edited for brevity.)

Joe, 22: Passive Young Professional

A software engineer in San Francisco, Joe is single and lives with two roommates in the Mission district. While he often complains about high rent, he loves living in the City.

Helping others is nice, but I won’t donate money because my rent is too high and I don’t know where my donations would go.

Maddie, 32: The Selective Helper

Maddie is a psychotherapist with an expertise in Mindfulness Meditation at UCSF. When she comes home from work on weekdays, she is emotionally exhausted from the day’s sessions.

I think I’m doing enough for my community with my counseling services.

Blake, 45: The Caring Mother

Blake is a stay-at-home mother to two children. Her primary concerns are for her immediate family, as she wants to take care of her growing children and parents.

I want my children to be compassionate towards all people, but I don’t know where to start.

  

The personas helped us refine the direction of our project, which led to a new problem statement:

How might we design a simple volunteer experience for people who have no prior experience with volunteering?

Making design decisions

We wanted to create a low-commitment and personalized volunteering experience to encourage our users to volunteer more. The ultimate far-reaching goal for the project was to build relationships with individuals in the homeless community.

The following design decisions were made to reach those goals:

Decision Reason
Mobile Application Reflect ease of volunteering with the portability of a mobile device
Gamification Encourage user retention by awarding badges and leveling statuses
Social Component Share current volunteering activities at organizations with friends

With a little over 12 hours left in the hackathon, I organized the information architecture and designed high-fidelity wireframes and prototypes, and provided design support for my team with the onboarding experience.

Information Architecture

I reduced the number of pages to three, hoping to encourage users to primarily interact with the Volunteer page. The Profile and Friends pages served as ways for users to track their progress and view their friends’ volunteer journeys.

The Onboarding Experience

We took inspiration from Duolingo, a language learning application, and its onboarding experience for new users. Duolingo’s onboarding process asks users to quickly assess their level of language mastery and outline their personal goals with using the application, personalizing their language learning experience.

Pictured: Comparison between Duolingo and our proposed onboarding experience

Our onboarding experience would ask new users to define their goals as volunteers. The goals of this experience were to set the expectation that volunteering is easy and to introduce the rewards volunteers can achieve by volunteering.

Solutions

My team’s ultimate goals for this project were to empower the volunteers with easily accessible resources and to empower the homeless through deepening relationships with consistent volunteers.

We presented our final solutions through three avenues: Partnership, Gamification, and Friends. I designed all of the following hi-fidelity screens.

Partnership

By eventually partnering with non-profit organizations, we hoped to directly connect volunteers with organizations through our mobile application and streamline the process of volunteers tracking their volunteering.

Pictured: Process of logging volunteer hours

Gamification

Gamifying the experience with achievements, status levels, and experience points gives extra incentives for users to continue volunteering and using the application. This would hopefully lead volunteers to develop the habit of voluntarily spending their time at charitable organizations.

Pictured: A volunteer's profile page with his or her list of achievements

Social

The friends feed would allow volunteers to see their friends’ status levels, recently acquired badges, and activities at local organizations. One decision we came to was omitting the option to like or comment on friends’ activities, as we did not want volunteerism to become a competition with other people.

Pictured: Social feed of friends' activities

In the remaining 8 hours of the hackathon, I designed a full prototype on Adobe XD.

Takeaways

•  Defining a target audience is essential for driving design decisions. Given that my team consisted mostly of people new to hackathons and design thinking, our initial ideations were broad, exciting, and perplexing. After I led my team to create user personas to better frame our problem, it was easier to narrow down our project scope.

•  Every single screen, design element, or interaction should contribute to the end goal of the user. The discussions my team had on gamification led to a few disagreements, but once I advocated for the user personas we had defined together, the gamification ideas become a secondary priority. Setting that expectation helped to simplify the user experience of the application.

•  And possibly the most important takeaway: It’s possible to sleep at hackathons!
