Valour SF is a mobile application that encourages people to volunteer at local homeless organizations in San Francisco. By gamifying the experience and providing an option to connect with friends online, this project aimed to be a stepping stone for people to begin their volunteerism journey.

This project was completed at a 24-hour hackathon in November 2019 (Hacking for Humanity with Girls in Tech SF) and received an honorable mention from the judges. I worked in a team with four other people, and taught design thinking principles to my team. I was the sole designer.

The Challenge

Hack for humanity.

The topic of the hackathon was social and civic issues in the San Francisco community. Our goal was to address one issue in San Francisco and design a solution for it. To spur ideas, a couple of representatives from different community organizations were invited to speak about their organizations’ respective missions.

Context & User Research

Homelessness is a big issue, so we decided to narrow our users to people who have the capacity to help.

One organization stood out to my team – the St. Anthony Foundation, an organization that provides free services to the homeless community in San Francisco. A couple of the services include a dining hall that serves over 2,000 meals each day and a free technology center that gives homeless individuals access to computers.

My team wanted to explore this issue of homelessness even further. With over 8,000 homeless individuals and 800,000 residents in San Francisco, I knew we needed to incorporate human compassion into our project solution to galvanize change.

We identified three groups involved in this space:

  • Homeless individuals
  • Non-profit organizations
  • Volunteers

My team preliminarily discussed a few project ideas, including an Airbnb-type service to connect homeless individuals with willing hosts, or a way to simplify the eviction process for individuals before they become homeless. While these ideas were worth exploring, we were concerned about the technology needed to access these services. Because of this, we shifted our users from homeless individuals to people who have the resources to contribute to the homeless community.

Ideation

The core value of this project was compassion. My team considered several project ideas to drive compassion for the homeless community.

The idea of fostering a relationship with homeless individuals would allow people to directly show compassion to the homeless community. We considered the idea of an independent sponsorship program to bridge these relationships, but ran into the problem of finding willing individuals to participate in the program.

We decided to leverage organizations like St. Anthony’s to form the foundations of these relationships. My team began considering ways to encourage people to volunteer with the end goal of fostering relationships with the people they would interact with.

Bouncing around ideas for this was difficult, as my team members and I had differing opinions on the solution. I suggested coming up with user personas to narrow down the scope and help direct our decisions for the project.

User Personas

I led my team to develop personas in order to guide our project.

My team was not familiar with human-centered design principles, so I led my team to create profiles for three individuals living in San Francisco. We defined general perspectives on volunteering and considered users who shared a similar sentiment of not previously being involved with volunteer programs.

  • Joe, 22
  • Maddie, 32
  • Blake, 45
The Passive Young Professional
  • Joe is a software engineer in San Francisco. He is single and lives with two roommates in the Mission district. While he often complains about high rent, he loves living in the City.
  • He leaves for work early and comes home late, so in his free time he likes to relax by playing Call of Duty with his roommates – sometimes, he goes out to bars.
  • Joe thinks helping others is nice, but doesn’t want to donate money because he thinks he doesn’t have enough (given his high rent) and is unsure about where the money will go. He also doesn’t have time during the week to physically volunteer.
The Selective Helper
  • Maddie is a psychotherapist with an expertise in Mindfulness Meditation at UCSF. When she comes home from work on weekdays, she is emotionally exhausted from the day’s sessions. She usually winds down by reading a book before bed.
  • She spends the weekends hanging out with friends, journaling at coffee shops, or preparing for her sessions in the upcoming week.
  • Maddie believes that she’s doing enough for the community with her counseling services and doesn’t think she needs to spend her free time helping non-profit organizations. If she were to volunteer her time, she would want to volunteer with minimal engagement.
The Caring Mother
  • Blake is a stay-at-home mother to two children, ages 9 and 10. She feels content with her life and is financially stable. Her primary concerns are for her immediate family, as she wants to take care of her growing children and parents.
  • She has compassion for the homeless community, and she contributes to her children’s canned food drives and often donates outgrown clothes to her local Goodwill.
  • Blake has lived a well-balanced life and wants her children to grow up with the same values she adopted over the years. She wants to encourage her children to also be compassionate for the homeless community but is overwhelmed with where to begin.
Reframing

How might we design a simple volunteer experience for people who have no prior experience with volunteering?

Defining the personas helped my team ideate an experience that felt personalized but was also low-commitment to encourage more volunteerism. The ultimate far-reaching goal for the project was to build relationships with individuals in the homeless community. (We didn’t consider user acquisition of our product, as we assumed that people using the application would be those already interested in finding out how to volunteer.)

Design Process

All of the design decisions were made to encourage more people to volunteer, ultimately encouraging volunteers to build relationships with homeless individuals.

The first decision I made was to create a mobile application in order to reflect the ease of volunteering. To encourage user retention with the application, some of my team members suggested that we to gamify the experience and allow users to earn badges and climb statuses as they continued to volunteer. As a team, we also decided to add a social component to the application so users would be able to see their friends’ activities at organizations, further encouraging them to volunteer on their own time.

With a little over 12 hours left in the hackathon, I organized the information architecture and designed high-fidelity wireframes and prototypes while providing design support for my team with the application onboarding experience.

Onboarding Designs

The onboarding experience aimed to give users an idea of how easy it is to begin their volunteer journeys.

We took inspiration from Duolingo, a language learning application, and its onboarding experience for new users. Duolingo’s onboarding process asks users to quickly assess their level of language mastery and outline their personal goals with using the application, personalizing their language learning experience.

In our onboarding experience, we asked new users about their goals for volunteering and categorized the types of contributions they could make (time, money, and items). By limiting the number of potential contributions users could make, we were setting the expectation that volunteering can be easy. We also introduced the gamification features to further validate and reward users for taking the first step to becoming a volunteer.

Taking the sketches that my team and I had created, I designed a high-fidelity prototype for the onboarding experience.

Information Architecture

With the goal of encouraging more volunteerism, I consolidated all the volunteer resources to a single page to empower users to volunteer.

By reducing the number of pages in the application to three main pages, I hoped to encourage users to primarily interact with the Volunteer page. The Profile and Friends pages served as ways for users to track their progress and view their friends’ volunteer journeys.

  • Volunteer
  • Friends
  • Profile
  • Considering the profiles of Joe and Blake (people who are unsure about how to help), I designed automatically generated tasks in the form of “quick tasks” for volunteers who are unsure of how to contribute to organizations.
  • Limiting the donation categories would promote the idea that volunteering can be easy.
  • To encourage volunteer retention at the same organizations, I included a list of organizations for volunteers to keep track of places they had previously volunteered or would like to volunteer with.
  • Social users like Maddie would find extra incentives to volunteer with this page, as volunteers would be able to view where their friends are volunteering.
  • We decided to not include the option for users to like or comment on friend activities, as we wanted to steer away from romanticizing volunteering.
  • All of the user’s status updates and achievements would live in the profile page. By displaying all of their achievements, we wanted to encourage users to continue volunteering to gain more points and earn more badges.
Solutions

We decided to name our application Valour SF to reflect our user’s empowerment and compassion gained from volunteering in the San Francisco community.

My team’s ultimate goal for this project was to empower the user through incentivizing volunteering and destigmatizing homeless shelter volunteering, and to empower the homeless through deepening relationships with consistent volunteers.

We presented our final solutions through four avenues: Partnership, Gamification, Friends, and Humanization. I designed all of the following screens.

  • Partnership
  • Gamification
  • Social
  • Humanization

By eventually partnering with non-profit organizations such as St. Anthony’s in San Francisco, we hoped to directly connect volunteers with organizations through our mobile application and streamline the process of volunteers signing up and tracking their volunteering. Features like the Time page would require volunteers to coordinate with the site supervisors and record their volunteer hours directly in the application. This feature would be useful for students who need volunteer hours, and would encourage volunteers to further foster relationships with those they meet at the organizations.

Gamifying the experience with achievements, status levels, and experience points gives extra incentives for users to continue volunteering and using the application. With each item or monetary donation, or time spent volunteering, volunteers would gain experience points (XP) to level up as a volunteer. This feature would hopefully lead volunteers to develop the habit of voluntarily spending their time at charitable organizations. I designed all the icons to represent the status levels of a volunteer.

The friends feed would allow volunteers to see their friends’ status levels, recently acquired badges, and activities. One decision we came to was omitting the option to like or comment on friends’ activities, as we did not want volunteerism to become a competition with other people.

Because this feature was one that was beyond the scope of our time at the hackathon, we filed away this idea as a future feature.

Considering our core value of compassion and our end goal of relationships, we wanted to design an experience that would allow volunteers to have a one-to-one relationship with homeless individuals at these local organizations, or form a sponsorship-like program with them. Because every individual has a different story, we wanted to create a space for understanding and empathy in these relationships.

In the remaining 8 hours of the hackathon, I designed the screens and full prototype for the mobile application.

Takeaways
  • Defining a target audience and user base is essential for driving design decisions. My team consisted of people who were new to hackathons and design thinking, so our initial ideations were broad, exciting, and perplexing. But after I led my team to create user personas to better frame our problem, it was easier to narrow down our scope. Personas also helped us to develop the goal of our project: to encourage new volunteers to begin their volunteer journeys and eventually build relationships with the people they served.
  • Every single screen, design element, or interaction should contribute to the end goal of the user. My team quickly became overwhelmed by the idea of gamification and had a few disagreements over how to approach gamifying the experience. However, I advocated for the user personas we had defined together and helped to reframe our project goal of encouraging volunteerism, so the gamification feature became a secondary priority. Setting that expectation helped to simplify the user experience of the application.
  • And a bonus takeaway: It’s possible to sleep at hackathons!

Designing this application from scratch was an exciting process. After being given the prompt to “hack for humanity,” it was easy for my team to jump between many project ideas, but guiding my team to think from the perspective of our users helped us to define our single project idea.

Given that this project was completed during a 24-hour hackathon, we were unable to complete any in-person user research on our initial idea and on the end prototype I designed. As a result, a lot of our design decisions were based on assumptions from personal experience and our user personas. If we were given more time, I would have also done deeper research on the effects of gamification on activity retention.

Participating in this hackathon was a great opportunity to work alongside strangers from different backgrounds who quickly became a cohesive team: two recent college graduates, one high school student, one engineer, and one mother. Being able to witness many of their first introductions to design thinking throughout the hackathon confirmed my passion for user experience design, and encourages me even more to continue advocating for the user in everything I design.
